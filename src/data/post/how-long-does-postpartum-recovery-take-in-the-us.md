<div class="bg-orange-50 border border-orange-200 rounded-xl p-6 my-8">
  <p class="text-lg font-semibold text-orange-800 mb-3">Six weeks is not the full story</p>
  <p class="mb-0">Many mothers hear that postpartum recovery takes 6 weeks. In reality, medical clearance and full recovery are not the same. This guide breaks down what usually improves in the first days, weeks, and months after birth.</p>
</div>

## At a Glance

<div class="grid md:grid-cols-3 gap-4 my-6">
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg shadow-sm border border-slate-200 p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-orange-700 mb-2">Medical Recovery</h4>
    <p class="text-sm mb-0">The basic 6-week check focuses on healing, bleeding, incision status, and whether normal activity can begin again.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg shadow-sm border border-slate-200 p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-orange-700 mb-2">Real Recovery</h4>
    <p class="text-sm mb-0">Energy, pelvic floor strength, sleep, body changes, and emotional stability often take 6 months to 1 year.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg shadow-sm border border-slate-200 p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-orange-700 mb-2">Most Helpful Mindset</h4>
    <p class="text-sm mb-0">Recovery is gradual, uneven, and different for every mother. Progress matters more than speed.</p>
  </div>
</div>

## 1. Medical Recovery vs. Actual Recovery

<div class="grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-4 my-6">
  <div class="bg-blue-50 border border-blue-200 rounded-lg p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-blue-700 mb-3">What doctors usually mean by 6 weeks</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Uterus shrinks back toward normal size</li>
      <li>Lochia greatly decreases or stops</li>
      <li>Perineal tears or C-section incision heal</li>
      <li>Light exercise and sex may be cleared</li>
      <li>Serious complications are checked</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-orange-50 border border-orange-200 rounded-lg p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-orange-700 mb-3">What recovery often still includes after that</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Pelvic floor weakness</li>
      <li>Core weakness and abdominal separation</li>
      <li>Sleep deprivation and exhaustion</li>
      <li>Hormonal changes and emotional swings</li>
      <li>Body image and identity adjustment</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

## 2. Timeline by Delivery Type

<div class="grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-4 my-6">
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg p-5 shadow-sm border-l-4 border-blue-400">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-blue-700 mb-3">Vaginal Birth</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Week 1: heavy bleeding, perineal pain, swelling, severe fatigue</li>
      <li>Weeks 2-4: pain improves, movement gets easier, but rest is still necessary</li>
      <li>Weeks 5-6: daily activity feels more manageable, though tiredness often remains</li>
      <li>3-6 months: pelvic floor, core strength, and stamina continue recovering</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg p-5 shadow-sm border-l-4 border-orange-400">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-orange-700 mb-3">C-Section</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Week 1: incision pain, difficulty standing, lifting, coughing, or laughing</li>
      <li>Weeks 2-4: surface healing improves, but overdoing it still causes setbacks</li>
      <li>Weeks 5-8: normal movement increases, but lifting and intense exercise stay limited</li>
      <li>3-6 months: deeper tissue healing continues long after the incision looks better</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

## 3. What Heals at Different Speeds

<div class="grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-4 my-6">
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg border border-slate-200 p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-teal-700 mb-3">Body Changes That Improve Early</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Uterine contraction and reduced bleeding</li>
      <li>Decreased swelling and less acute pain</li>
      <li>Better walking, sitting, and standing tolerance</li>
      <li>Basic incision or tear healing</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg border border-slate-200 p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-purple-700 mb-3">Body Changes That Usually Take Longer</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Pelvic floor recovery and bladder control</li>
      <li>Abdominal strength and diastasis recti improvement</li>
      <li>Breast changes with feeding or weaning</li>
      <li>Weight, body shape, and stamina recovery</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="bg-amber-50 border border-amber-200 rounded-lg p-5 my-6">
  <p class="text-sm mb-2"><strong>Reality check:</strong> many mothers still feel physically different at 3 months, 6 months, and even 1 year postpartum.</p>
  <p class="text-sm mb-0"><strong>That does not mean something is wrong.</strong> It usually means recovery is still ongoing.</p>
</div>

## 4. Emotional Recovery, Sleep, and Energy

<div class="grid md:grid-cols-3 gap-4 my-6">
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg shadow-sm p-5 border-t-4 border-blue-400">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-blue-700 mb-3">First 2 Weeks</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Baby blues are common</li>
      <li>Crying and mood swings may increase</li>
      <li>Sleep is highly fragmented</li>
      <li>Fatigue can feel extreme</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg shadow-sm p-5 border-t-4 border-orange-400">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-orange-700 mb-3">2 Weeks to 3 Months</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Adjustment stress is still high</li>
      <li>Confidence builds slowly</li>
      <li>Sleep deprivation accumulates</li>
      <li>Support makes a major difference</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-lg shadow-sm p-5 border-t-4 border-teal-400">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-teal-700 mb-3">3 Months and Beyond</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Routine often becomes more stable</li>
      <li>Energy gradually improves</li>
      <li>Emotional balance becomes easier</li>
      <li>Sleep may still take many months to normalize</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

## 5. Exercise, Intimacy, and Returning to Normal Life

<div class="space-y-4 my-6">
  <div class="bg-white rounded-xl border border-slate-200 overflow-hidden">
    <div class="bg-orange-500 px-5 py-3 text-white font-bold text-sm">Exercise</div>
    <div class="p-5 text-sm">
      <p>Light walking, breathing work, and gentle movement may begin early if your provider agrees. More structured exercise usually waits until after the 6-week visit, and high-impact movement often needs more time.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-xl border border-slate-200 overflow-hidden">
    <div class="bg-teal-600 px-5 py-3 text-white font-bold text-sm">Sexual Recovery</div>
    <div class="p-5 text-sm">
      <p>Medical clearance does not always mean emotional or physical readiness. Pain, dryness, fear, fatigue, and body discomfort are common. Slow communication with your partner matters more than a timeline.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-white rounded-xl border border-slate-200 overflow-hidden">
    <div class="bg-blue-500 px-5 py-3 text-white font-bold text-sm">Work and Social Return</div>
    <div class="p-5 text-sm">
      <p>Some mothers return to work at 6 to 12 weeks, but many still feel physically tired and emotionally stretched. Practical support, pumping plans, childcare, and realistic expectations are essential.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

## 6. When to Call Your Doctor

<div class="grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-4 my-6">
  <div class="bg-red-50 border border-red-200 rounded-lg p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-red-700 mb-3">Physical warning signs</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Fever over 100.4 F</li>
      <li>Heavy bleeding that suddenly increases</li>
      <li>Bad-smelling discharge</li>
      <li>Severe abdominal pain</li>
      <li>Shortness of breath, severe headache, or leg swelling</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-red-50 border border-red-200 rounded-lg p-5">
    <h4 class="font-bold text-red-700 mb-3">Emotional warning signs</h4>
    <ul class="text-sm space-y-1 mb-0">
      <li>Depression that lasts more than 2 weeks</li>
      <li>Inability to care for yourself or baby</li>
      <li>Severe panic or anxiety</li>
      <li>Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

## 7. The Most Realistic Expectation

<div class="bg-slate-50 rounded-xl border border-slate-200 p-6 my-8">
  <p class="mb-3">Postpartum recovery is rarely a straight line. You may feel stronger one week and exhausted the next.</p>
  <p class="mb-3">That is normal. What matters is steady recovery, enough support, and responding early when something feels off.</p>
  <p class="mb-0"><strong>Good recovery is not about “bouncing back.”</strong> It is about healing safely and building a stable new routine.</p>
</div>
